IN THE OLD FOLK’S HOME IN MOYSHEV Z'KAYNIM
By E. Kaganovski

“Once again…Your mother’s oldest brother was called Yossel, the younger one was called Mayshel, and the third?”

“The third was a step-brother from the first husband. The first husband was called Khayim, a tall Yid. He had the large paper shop in the market – in the very center of the market!”

“Well, that I really can’t remember…I was just a child then, but Uncle Yossel, I do remember. I recall he was a tobacco dealer, perhaps snuff also.”

“Ah, there were two brothers who handled tobacco, my uncles from my father’s side; so, I think you are mistaken.”

“What do you mean – I’m mistaken? I didn’t know Yossel the tobacconist?
A yellowish Yid with a beard, the color of tobacco.”

“Yes, yes…”

“So what are you saying, that I don’t remember? He was my mother Elye-Shloime’s brother!”

“No, no that was my father’s brother, who was my aunt’s second husband. Her first husband was Yehoshua, he was actually a Sarver.”

“Well, now I am really confused, excuse me, but you don’t know what you’re talking about. Yehoshua Sarver was my friend, you understand. How could I not remember Yehoshua Sarver?”

“How could you possibly know him, since at that time I was a young boy, and he was at least sixty? I myself can barely remember him!”

“So, if you have such a head - who is to blame? I myself, as you see, can remember everything, as if it were happening now. I remember that when your brother Isaac married Reb Shloime’s daughter from Lublin, they gave out fresh rolls to everyone on the street!”

“My brother, Isaac? I never had a brother Isaac!”

“You certainly had a brother Isaac! Wait a moment….what was his business? Yes, he was in leather . He had a leather shop.’

“Leather? None of us ever dealt in leather. We all dealt with oil or kerosene.”

“Wait, I know who you mean….you mean the Isaac who dealt with iron, he traded in iron! He lived on the same street as we did, but oy! He had a tragic death! He choked on a bone in the middle of a Friday night meal! Vay,vay, the whole town mourned him!’

“So what you’re saying is no? Alright then…..no is no. Perhaps it doesn’t suit you to tell me who you are?”

“What do you mean; I don’t want to tell you? What do you mean?? I tell you that I am from the Tenenboims….and your family was well known to us.” “I recall, as if it were only yesterday, how I would come into your father’s shop – may he rest in peace - for a bottle of wine, for Shabbat or Yom Tov. He loved to recite from the good books; he always had a good word.’’

“How could you possibly have come to my father for wine? My father had no wine. My father-in-law sold wine. It was about half a year after the wedding, when the dowry was finally given. How did you get to my father and wine, when his whole life he sold lime and whitewash?

“What do you mean? That wine is dirtier, God forbid, than lime? Secondly, what does it matter what a Jew deals in, at least it’s a living! But even worse, it simply doesn’t suit a man of your years to weave such a story! Wait, I will soon remind you…sha..sha...Your mother was a tall woman, a nice-looking woman, good as gold, such a sweet soul…”

“Yes, yes, that is true.”

“And she certainly was called Dobele?”

“Certainly not! My mother - may she rest in peace – was Miryam…yes, that’s what she was called…Miryam.”

“Akh…enough already. You will soon tell me that her father, your grandfather, was not called Reb Aharon, and did not deal in glass…”

“Certainly not!”

“Then you are, you’ll excuse me for saying, an old fool!”

“And you are a cow!”

*

Both old people sat on a long wooden bench, like children, in the dried-out, thinning garden of the moshav zkaynim – the old folks’ home - with small, clenched, angry looks on their dried-apricot faces, warily eyeing each other.

“It is impossible to speak with you…your head is all muddled…you have forgotten everything!”

“And you are going to tell me what MY mother - may she rest in peace - was called?? Who ever heard of such a thing?!”

After a long silence, one turns to the other:

“How old are you?”

“What is it to you?”

“Are you afraid to tell me?”

“I am old….let me see….around sixty-eight…”

“The same as me!”

“Nu, so you see? You should be able to remember!”

“I actually do remember…”

“Sha, wait a moment….I will ask you something that you surely must know:
What was the old Rebbe called?”

“What was the old Rebbe’s name? Who doesn’t know? Rev Zeinvele!”

“Reb Zeinvele, he says. Oy, this Jew is indeed crazy! Listen to me, and don’t take this to heart…..but I am beginning to think that you are not even from Radomer…”

“Who says I am from Radomer?”

“What! You are not from Radomer?”

“Certainly not!”

“So why are you going around telling me tales?”

And with that, the two old people angrily turned their backs on one another.
